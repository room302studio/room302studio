# Coaching Subway Builder: From Broken Train to Launched Game

In October 2024, studio member Colin Miller launched [Subway Builder](https://www.subwaybuilder.com), a hyperrealistic transit simulation game that uses U.S. Census data to simulate millions of commuters across 26 cities. Within weeks, the game was featured in Fast Company, Planetizen, NY1, and multiple transit publications.

This is the story of how we helped Colin take his idea from concept to successful launch—and what we learned about our 0-60 incubation model along the way.

## The Origin: A Broken 6 Train

Colin's idea came from the most relatable place: being stuck on a broken-down 6 train during his commute. Instead of just complaining about the MTA, he asked himself: "What would it take to actually build a better transit system?"

That curiosity turned into a weekend prototype. The prototype turned into an obsession. And that's when he brought it to the studio.

## Our Role: More Than Just Feedback

When Colin first showed us early prototypes, we knew this had potential. But potential isn't enough—especially for a solo developer working on an ambitious simulation game while holding down a day job.

We provided three things:

### 1. UX Iteration and Design Feedback

Transit data is inherently complex. Pathfinding algorithms, passenger flows, budget constraints—how do you make that accessible without dumbing it down?

We ran regular design reviews, stress-tested prototypes, and helped Colin find the balance between realism and playability. The goal wasn't to make it simple—it was to make complexity feel manageable.

Key questions we kept asking:
- "What does a new player need to understand in the first 5 minutes?"
- "Where do players get stuck and give up?"
- "How can we communicate failure in a way that teaches?"

### 2. Engineering Management and Workflows

Building a game solo means you're also your own project manager, QA team, and release engineer. That's a lot of hats.

We helped Colin:
- Set up sustainable development workflows
- Implement version control best practices
- Create a realistic roadmap that balanced ambition with shippable milestones
- Break down overwhelming features into manageable tasks

The critical insight: A launched game with 80% of features beats a perfect game that never ships.

### 3. Community Building and Discord Moderation

As Colin built in public and started attracting early adopters, we helped establish and moderate the Discord server.

We structured channels for:
- Beta testing feedback
- Bug reports and feature requests
- Technical support
- Community discussion

The Discord became more than a support channel—it became a feedback loop. Colin could test ideas, gather reactions, and iterate based on real player needs.

We also facilitated connections with other developers who could help with specific technical challenges, from pathfinding optimization to cross-platform compatibility.

## What We Didn't Do

Importantly, we didn't:
- Write code for the game
- Handle press outreach or launch strategy
- Make design decisions for Colin

Our role was to **remove friction** and **provide structure**, not to take over. Colin remained the sole creative and technical lead. This was his vision—we just helped him execute it faster and better.

## The Results

October 2024: Subway Builder launches on Windows, macOS, and Linux.

Within a month:
- [Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91421609/this-addictive-game-is-like-simcity-but-for-transit-nerds) called it "addictive"
- [Planetizen](https://www.planetizen.com/news/2025/10/136214-nerd-alert-subway-builder-video-game-lets-you-build-your-dream-transit-system) featured it for urban planners
- [NY1](https://ny1.com/nyc/all-boroughs/CTV/2025/09/20/subway-builder-allows-players-to-design-their-own-transit-system) covered the NYC angle
- Transit advocacy communities embraced it as a planning tool
- Casual gamers found it as a SimCity alternative

## Lessons for Studio Incubation

This project validated our belief that great individual developers don't need someone to build things for them—they need:

**Structured feedback loops**: Regular check-ins keep momentum without micromanaging

**Project management infrastructure**: Roadmaps and workflows prevent burnout

**Community scaffolding**: Early community building creates accountability and motivation

**Permission to ship imperfect**: Helping set realistic scope prevents perfectionism paralysis

## The 0-60 Model in Practice

Subway Builder proves our thesis: With the right support structure, talented developers can go from concept to successful launch without giving up creative control or equity.

Colin maintained 100% ownership of his vision, code, and revenue. We provided the framework, feedback, and community support to help him execute faster and better.

That's what Room 302 Studio does best: We help creators get from 0 to 60 by removing friction, not by taking the wheel.
